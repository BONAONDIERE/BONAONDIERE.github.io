About

The engineer who
became a data analyst

An unusual path — from circuits and sensors to pipelines and models. That engineering foundation is exactly what makes the difference.

I started in electronics because I wanted to understand how things work at the lowest level. Working on IoT systems — with 500+ connected devices sending constant data — I realised that the hardware was never the interesting part. The data it produced was. That's when everything shifted.

Today I work end-to-end: engineering ETL pipelines, performing statistical analysis, building and deploying ML models, and designing dashboards that non-technical stakeholders actually use. I've processed millions of IoT records with PySpark, built Power BI and Tableau dashboards cutting reporting time by 40%, and built predictive models in production — not just notebooks sitting in a GitHub repo.

My background in engineering means I think in systems — I understand how data is generated at the source, what can go wrong in a pipeline, and why data quality problems happen before they become dashboard problems. That's a perspective most data analysts don't have.

I'm pursuing a Master's in AI and Technology Management at Nexford University (fully remote, Washington DC), and I hold an AWS Cloud Practitioner certification and a World Bank-sponsored Data Science qualification. I'm looking for a fully remote data role where I can do meaningful work on a global team.
